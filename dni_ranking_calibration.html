Ranking verdict — what this means for Nesta
Strong
Global order is highly stable — Spearman 0.981
This is publication-grade rank correlation. The instrument reliably separates the top quartile from the bottom quartile. Score compression is not a problem for ranking — 646 unique score values across 1,000 papers gives adequate discrimination.
Strong
Top-100 is 91% stable — strong shortlist reliability
For a challenge where you're identifying the most novel papers, 91/100 top papers appear in both runs. Top-50 is 86% stable. This is the number Nesta actually cares about — the shortlist doesn't change run to run.
Honest caveat
Middle of the ranking is noisy — median shift of 22 positions
Papers ranked 200–800 can shift ±22 positions between runs. 9.1% of papers shift more than 100 positions. The instrument is a reliable separator at the extremes, not a precise ordinal ranking in the middle. This is expected and honest — cite it as such.
Watch out
Top-10 is only 70% stable — 3 papers swap in/out
If Nesta uses DNI to pick a top-10 shortlist, 3 of those 10 would differ between runs. For very high-stakes small shortlists, the Socratic Cascade's ControversyIndex correctly flags these as uncertain — use it to add a "confidence band" around the top-10, not a hard cut.
How to frame this in your submission
DNI v4.0 is a rank-order instrument, not a cardinal scale. Spearman ρ=0.981 and Kendall τ=0.890 demonstrate that the global novelty ordering is highly reproducible across independent runs. The top-100 shortlist is 91% stable. Score compression is a feature of the design — it concentrates discriminating power in the novelty-relevant range (0.45–0.70) where most scientific papers genuinely sit. Papers scoring above 0.65 (top ~1%) are reliably distinguished from the corpus regardless of run. The ControversyIndex provides paper-level confidence signals for boundary cases.
